# High-Converting Checkout Pages

Your checkout page is where the money is made or lost. A page can pull traffic, warm people up, and still leak most of its sales at the final step. This is a plain, actionable guide to building a checkout that converts — and a pre-launch checklist to run before you send a single visitor.

---

## Use a Two-Step Order Form (and Why)

A two-step order form splits checkout into two screens:

- **Step 1:** name and email (the opt-in).
- **Step 2:** payment details, order bumps, and the buy button.

**Never run a funnel without the opt-in step.** Here's why it matters more than it looks:

- **60-70% of people who start checkout never finish.** With the email captured in step 1, you can send an abandoned-cart follow-up and recover some of them. Without it, they're gone with no way to reach them.
- **It grows your list.** Every step-1 completion is an email you own, whether or not they buy today.
- **It gives you a clean conversion number to optimize** (see the step-2 benchmark below).

A one-page "show everything like an open sales page" form removes both the recovery path and the list growth. Don't use it.

---

## Where to Place Proof

Low checkout conversion is very often a **missing-proof** problem. People got to the page, then hesitated because nothing reassured them at the moment of paying.

- Put **2-3 short testimonial snippets right above the order bumps**, where the eye lands before the buy button.
- Keep each quote to **1-2 sentences.** Long testimonials get skipped.
- Use **specific results** where you have them ("landed 3 clients in the first week" beats "great program").
- Add a **photo** with the quote if you have one — it raises trust.
- Repeat your **guarantee** near the buy button so the last thing they read is "risk-free."

Only use real quotes. Don't invent testimonials or numbers — an empty proof slot is better than a fake one, and easier to fix later.

---

## Remove Friction Fields

Every field on the checkout is a chance to lose the sale. Strip anything not strictly required to charge the card:

- **Remove the phone field.** You don't need it to deliver a digital product, and it adds hesitation.
- **Remove the coupon/discount code box.** An empty coupon field makes buyers stop, open a new tab, and go hunting for a code — many never come back. If you're not running a specific coupon promo, take it off entirely.
- **Cut address fields** for digital products. You're not shipping anything.
- **Keep it to:** name, email, card. That's the whole required set for most low-ticket digital offers.

---

## How Bumps Affect Checkout Conversion

Order bumps raise your average order value — that's the point. But they can also **drag checkout conversion down** if they're handled wrong:

- If a bump makes the main product feel incomplete ("I guess I need this too"), buyers get uneasy and some abandon. Keep bumps positioned as optional next steps or convenience, never as required.
- Too many bumps clutter the page and slow the decision. Two or three, cleanly presented, is plenty.
- If checkout conversion drops when you add a bump, the fix is usually the **bump copy or positioning**, not removing bumps altogether.

The goal is the balance: bumps lift AOV enough that the small dip (if any) in checkout rate is more than paid for.

---

## The ~30% Step-2 Benchmark

On a two-step order form, **step 2 should convert around 30%** — meaning roughly 30% of people who complete step 1 (opt-in) go on to complete the purchase.

- **At or above ~30%:** healthy. Focus your energy elsewhere.
- **Below 20%:** something on the checkout is leaking. Do these in order:
  1. Reposition proof — move testimonials above the bumps, add the guarantee near the button.
  2. Strip friction fields — kill phone and coupon boxes, cut anything not required.
  3. Re-check bump positioning — make sure no bump implies the main product won't work alone.

Track this in Stripe or GHL, not in Meta — ad platforms over-attribute and won't give you the true checkout number.

---

## A note on testing

At low volume, you can change a few things at once — you're hunting for big swings, not 2% improvements. The strict "change one thing at a time" discipline only matters once you're at roughly 500-1,000 sessions a day. Early on, fix the obvious leaks fast.

---

## Pre-Launch Checkout Checklist

Run this before you send traffic:

- [ ] Two-step order form is live (opt-in on step 1, payment + bumps on step 2)
- [ ] Abandoned-cart follow-up is connected to the step-1 opt-in
- [ ] Phone field removed
- [ ] Coupon/discount code field removed (unless a specific promo is running)
- [ ] Address/shipping fields removed (digital product)
- [ ] Only name, email, and card are required
- [ ] 2-3 short testimonials placed above the order bumps (real quotes only)
- [ ] Guarantee restated near the buy button
- [ ] Order bumps positioned as optional (nothing implies the main product is incomplete)
- [ ] No more than 2-3 bumps on the page
- [ ] Buy button text is clear and specific (not just "Submit")
- [ ] Page loads fast and works on mobile (most traffic is mobile)
- [ ] Test purchase completed end to end (card charges, receipt sends, product delivers)
- [ ] Conversion tracking fires on purchase (so you can measure step-2 rate in Stripe/GHL)

Get the leaks out first. Then send traffic. Test, validate, build.
