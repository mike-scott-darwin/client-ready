# Order Bump Profit Maximizer & Library

An order bump is the small offer on your checkout page — a checkbox next to your main product. Done right, it lifts your average order value without adding a single new visitor. Done wrong, it drags down the whole checkout.

This guide gives you the one rule that keeps bumps working, the logic for choosing what to sell, and a swipe file of copy templates you can fill in and paste.

---

## The One Rule for Bumps

**A bump must never feel required to get results from the main purchase.**

The moment a buyer senses "this won't actually work unless I also grab the bump," two things happen: they trust the main offer less, and they take the bump less. You lose both. So the rule holds the line — the main product is complete on its own.

A bump is allowed to do exactly one of two things:

1. **Add convenience.** You *can* do it yourself with the main product. The bump makes it faster, easier, or done-for-you.
2. **Solve the next problem** that naturally shows up once the main product solves the first one.

That's the whole test. If a bump idea does neither — or if it quietly implies the main thing is incomplete — cut it.

---

## The "Problem → Solution → Next Problem" Logic

Every good bump sits one step down the road from the thing they just bought. Map it like this:

- **Problem:** what the buyer walked in with.
- **Solution:** the main product.
- **Next problem:** what naturally comes up once the first problem is handled.
- **Bump:** the solution to that next problem (or a convenience layer on the main one).

Here's how the Client Ready funnel already maps:

| Step | Solves | Next problem that appears | Bump that answers it |
|------|--------|---------------------------|----------------------|
| Main ($47 Offer System) | "I don't have a clear offer" | "I have an offer — now how do I start conversations?" | DM Scripts ($37) |
| Bump 1 (DM Scripts) | "How do I start conversations?" | "I don't want to build every asset from scratch" | Templates ($67) |
| Bump 2 (Templates) | "I have the assets" | "Now how do I actually close?" | First $5K Client Playbook ($97) |

Notice: each bump is a real next step, not a missing piece. You could stop at any rung and still get the result you paid for. That's the rule working.

**How to invent your own bumps:** write down the main product's result, then ask "what's the very next thing this person will want to do?" That answer is your next-problem bump. Then ask "what part of the main product could be faster or done-for-them?" That's your convenience bump. You rarely need more than two or three.

## Swipe File: Order Bump Copy Templates

Fill in the brackets. Keep it to a headline, one to three lines of body, and a plain checkbox label. Sell the outcome they already want — not the category ("audit," "toolkit," "system") they aren't searching for.

The strongest pattern bakes the money-back guarantee straight into the headline, because every low-ticket product carries one anyway. Use it as a signal: mine is good enough to promise on.

### Direct-response + guarantee-baked-in (lead with these)

**1.**
> **Add [product] and [specific result] in [timeframe] — or your money back.**
> You just got [main product]. This gets you [next step] without the guesswork. [One line on what's inside.]
> Checkbox: *Yes, add [product] for $[price] — [result] or refunded.*

**2.**
> **Skip the blank page: [specific result] in [timeframe], or you don't pay.**
> [Main product] shows you what to do. This hands you the [templates/scripts/frameworks] so you [outcome] today.
> Checkbox: *Add [product] — $[price], money-back if it doesn't [result].*

**3.**
> **Use [product] and turn your next [N] [DMs/leads/pages] into [outcome] — or refunded.**
> [One line: what it removes / how it speeds them up.]
> Checkbox: *Yes, add [product] for $[price].*

### Next-problem bumps

**4.**
> **You've got [what main product gives them]. Now [next action].**
> [Product] gives you the exact [scripts/steps] to [next result] — so you don't stall right after you start.
> Checkbox: *Add [product] for $[price].*

**5.**
> **The step right after [main product result]: [next problem, phrased as a want].**
> [Product] handles [next problem] so momentum doesn't die. [One line on the deliverable.]
> Checkbox: *Yes, add [product] — $[price].*

**6.**
> **[Main product] gets you clear. This gets you [next outcome].**
> [One line on what's inside and who it's for.]
> Checkbox: *Add [product] for $[price].*

### Convenience bumps

**7.**
> **Want [main product result] done faster? Add [product].**

> Same result, less time. [Product] hands you [pre-built assets] so you [outcome] without starting cold.

**11.**

## How to Price a Bump

- **Don't be afraid to price up.** A bump that won't sell at $12 can sell at $24 — the price signals the value. Test the higher number before you assume the bump is broken.
- **But price won't rescue a weak offer.** If buyers won't take it at $27, they won't take it at $47. Price is a lever, not a fix.
- **Keep bumps under the main product's price** so the main purchase still feels like the anchor. Across the Client Ready checkout, bumps sit at $37 / $67 / $97 against the $47 front end and climb by next-step value.
- **Read the take-rate, not your gut.** If you get 20-30 sales and only about 1 person takes the bump, the offer isn't good enough — replace it, don't just re-price it. A well-matched bump can run 25% take-rate or higher; the target across all bumps combined is roughly half of buyers taking at least one.

**Bottom line:** keep the main product complete, put the next step (or a faster version of the current step) behind the checkbox, and write the headline as a promise you're willing to guarantee. Test the price up before you kill the bump. Let's go.
