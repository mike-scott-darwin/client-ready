# Miles Stutz Offer Optimizations (3 Decisions)

Three decisions from synthesizing Miles Stutz's No-Phone Offer Training and Digital Snack Hot Seat against the current Client Ready offer.

---

## Decision 1: Test $9 Front-End Price Point

### Situation

Current front-end is $27. Miles Stutz (Feb 2026) says $9 is current best practice for digital snacks: "Below $10 when you start, that's what we currently have best practices with."

Miles himself has 0.5-1.5% front-end conversion rate (below the 3% floor, far below Hernan's 7% target) but achieves 1.2 ROAS because 3 order bumps carry the offer.

### Options

#### Option A: Test $9 alongside $27 (selected)

Run $9 as a split test. Keep $27 as control. Measure conversion rate and AOV differences.

**Pros:**
- Lower friction = higher conversion volume
- More people enter funnel (more bump/OTO opportunities)
- Aligns with Miles' current best practice data
- Bumps ($17 + $37 + $67 = $121 max) still carry the model
- Max cart: $824 vs $842 — negligible difference

**Cons:**
- Perceived value may drop
- Existing $27 buyers may feel overpaid (unlikely — different traffic)
- Need to monitor AOV carefully

#### Option B: Drop to $9 permanently (rejected)

Go all-in on $9 without testing.

**Pros:** Simpler, no split test management
**Cons:** No data comparing the two. Risky without validation.

#### Option C: Stay at $27 (rejected)

Ignore Miles' data.

**Pros:** Known quantity
**Cons:** Ignoring current market data from someone spending real money on ads

### Decision

**Test $9 as a variant.** Run both price points with identical traffic. $300 per variant. Compare conversion rate AND AOV (including bumps). Winner takes all.

If $9 converts 2x+ higher than $27 with comparable AOV after bumps → switch permanently.

### What Changes

offer.md gets an A/B test note under Front-End pricing: "$9 test variant running alongside $27 control." No permanent change until data is in.

## Decision 2: Reframe Product as Case Study

### Situation

Current $27 product is a prompt system + template + guide. Functional but abstract. Miles says the best digital snack products are reverse-engineered case studies: "Take a problem you solved for a client that generated money. Reverse engineer what you did. Create a case study. That's the best thing you can do because it's pragmatic and practical. And it directly infuses proof."

### Options

#### Option A: Lead with case study, embed prompts (selected)

Restructure the front-end product to lead with a real story of a coach going through the Client Ready process. The 5 AI prompts become tools within the narrative, not the product itself.

**Structure:**
1. The Story — "Here's how [coach] went from [stuck] to [outcome] using this exact process"
2. The Framework — Why this worked (the Client Ready Method)
3. The Tools — The 5 AI Prompts (same content, recontextualized)
4. The Template — Offer document template (same)
5. Your Turn — Step-by-step walkthrough

**Pros:**
- Proof baked into the product (not just claims on the sales page)
- More concrete — reader follows a real journey
- Differentiates from generic "AI prompt" products flooding the market
- Same core content, different framing

**Cons:**
- Needs a real case study to lead with (Michael's own story works, or a client's)
- Slightly more work to restructure
- Must anonymize or get permission if using client story

#### Option B: Keep current format (rejected)

Leave product as-is.

**Pros:** Already built
**Cons:** Misses the proof-infusion opportunity. Competes with generic AI prompt products.

### Decision

**Reframe as case study.** Lead with Michael's own journey (7 years searching, found alignment) as the primary case study. The prompts become "here's the exact tools I used." When client case studies are available, add those too.

This isn't a rewrite — it's a restructure. Same 5 prompts, same template, same guide. Different frame: story first, tools second.

### What Changes

front-end-client-ready-offer-system.md gets restructured: Part 1 becomes the case study narrative, Part 2 recontextualizes prompts as "the tools behind the story," Parts 3-4 stay the same. offer.md front-end description updates to reflect case-study framing.

## Decision 3: Adopt "No-Phone Offer" Positioning Language

### Situation

The Client Ready system helps coaches sell without sales calls. But the current positioning doesn't name this outcome explicitly. Miles' "No-Phone Offer" framework gives a sharper angle: coaches who are tired of sales calls can build offers that sell themselves.

The $5K Accelerator already uses the 4 No-Phone Offer principles. But the front-end doesn't reference this language at all.

### Options

#### Option A: Use as outcome language, not brand (selected)

Frame "No-Phone Offer" as the OUTCOME the Client Ready system delivers. Not your brand — the result.

**Example copy:**
- "Build an offer so clear it sells without a sales call"
- "The No-Phone Offer: what your coaching becomes after extraction"
- "Stop selling on calls. Start selling on clarity."

**Pros:**
- Named outcome is more concrete than "validate your offer"
- Taps into real pain (sales calls eating 50-70% of time)
- Differentiates from "offer creation" products (there are hundreds)
- Doesn't copy Miles' brand — uses the concept as a category

**Cons:**
- "No-Phone Offer" is Miles' coined term — using it too prominently could feel derivative
- Audience may not know the term yet (needs education)

#### Option B: Create your own term for the same concept (considered)

Coin something like "Self-Closing Offer" or "Clarity Offer."

**Pros:** Original IP
**Cons:** Loses the recognition from Miles' audience (who are your audience too)

#### Option C: Don't use the language (rejected)

Keep current "validate your offer" positioning.

**Pros:** Safe
**Cons:** Misses the sharpest angle. "Validate" is process-focused. "No-Phone" is outcome-focused.

### Decision

**Use "No-Phone Offer" as outcome language, not brand.** Reference it as a concept/category, not as your product name. Lead with the pain ("sales calls eat 50-70% of your time") and position the Client Ready system as how you BUILD a No-Phone Offer.

The $5K Accelerator landing page already uses the 4 principles. Bring that language forward to the front-end positioning too.

**Specific language to test in ads:**
- "Build a No-Phone Offer — sell $5K+ without sales calls"
- "Your offer should close without you on the phone"
- "Stop selling on calls. Start selling on clarity."

### What Changes

audience.md gets a new pain point section: "stuck on sales calls" with Miles' market data (harder to book, more expensive, 50-70% time drain). offer.md headline and positioning language updated to reference no-phone/no-call outcome. Ad copy angles updated in proof/angles/ with no-phone messaging.
