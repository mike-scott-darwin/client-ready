**Subject line:** The validation shortcut nobody talks about

---

Most coaches spend weeks perfecting an offer before showing it to a single person.

They tweak the name. They redesign the logo. They agonize over pricing for three days. They build a landing page, write email sequences, set up automations.

Then they launch. Crickets.

Wrong approach. Completely backwards.

Here's what I've learned after seven years of building offers — most of them the hard way: **the fastest path to a working offer is not building one.** It's testing one.

---

I want to tell you about something I watched happen last month.

A guy in the community — let's call him James — had been sitting on an offer idea for four months. He had a Google Doc with 3,000 words of notes. He'd mapped out a 6-week curriculum. He'd even started recording course videos.

Zero sales. Zero conversations. Zero validation that anyone wanted what he was building.

When I asked him why he hadn't put it in front of anyone yet, he said what most people say: "I want to make sure it's ready."

Ready for what? For people who don't know it exists?

Here's what we did instead. Took about 45 minutes.

**Step 1: Strip the offer down to one sentence.** Not a tagline. Not a mission statement. One sentence that answers: what does the buyer get, and what does it cost? James went from a 3,000-word doc to: "I help operations managers build SOPs that cut onboarding time in half — $2,500 for a 4-week engagement."

**Step 2: Send it to five people who already know him.** Not strangers. Not cold DMs. Five people from his network who fit the profile. The message was simple: "Hey, I'm building something new. Can I get your honest take on this?" Then the one-sentence offer.

**Step 3: Listen.** Not pitch. Listen. Two of the five said "that's interesting but I don't need it right now." One didn't reply. One said "what exactly do you mean by SOPs?" And one said "how fast can you start?"

That last response is the only data point that matters.

James had a paying client within a week. Not from a funnel. Not from a landing page. From a single sentence and a conversation.

The four months of building? Wasted time. Not because the work was bad — because it was premature. He was solving problems he didn't have yet while ignoring the one problem that actually mattered: does anyone want this?

---

This is the pattern I see constantly. Smart people with real expertise, stuck in what I call **perfection as procrastination.**

Fourteen tabs open. Three courses half-finished. A Notion board that looks like mission control. And still no offer live.

The obsession with getting it right masks something else entirely. Fear of making the call. Fear of hearing "no." Fear of finding out the thing you spent four months on doesn't work.

But here's what nobody tells you about hearing "no."

A "no" after one week of testing costs you one week. A "no" after four months of building costs you four months. Same answer. Wildly different price.

Validation isn't about avoiding failure. It's about making failure cheap.

---

The shortcut is embarrassingly simple. I almost hesitate to write it because it doesn't sound like a "system." But it works better than anything complicated I've ever tried.

**Test the offer before you build the offer.**

That means:

1. **Write one sentence that describes the transformation and the price.** If you can't do this, the offer isn't clear yet. That's fine — it's a sign to keep thinking, not keep building.

2. **Put it in front of 5-10 people from your warm network.** Not your mom. People who could actually be buyers or who know people who could. Ask for honest feedback. Mean it.

3. **Track the responses.** You're looking for one specific signal: does anyone ask how to buy? Not "that's cool." Not "interesting idea." Does anyone reach for their wallet?

4. **If yes — build.** Now the landing page matters. Now the email sequence matters. Now you're building infrastructure for something people actually want.

5. **If no — adjust and retest.** Change the audience. Change the price. Change the transformation. Then send it to five more people. This loop costs you days, not months.

The whole thing takes an afternoon. Maybe two.

Compare that to the alternative: spending three months building a funnel for an offer nobody wants. That's not strategy. That's hope dressed up as work.
